# Artist Wellness — The Life Behind the Music

Health insurance, mental health, burnout prevention, and building a life that sustains the career.

## When to Activate

- "How do I get health insurance?" "I can't afford insurance"
- Artist sounds burned out, overwhelmed, or discouraged
- "I'm stressed about money" "Is this even worth it?"
- Proactive: it's been a while since checking in on the human behind the artist
- "I need to get my life together so I can focus on music"

## Health Insurance for Musicians

### Option 1: Healthcare.gov Marketplace (ACA)

Most independent artists qualify for subsidized insurance:

- **Open enrollment:** November 1 — January 15 (annually)
- **Special enrollment:** Triggered by life events (moving, losing coverage, turning 26)
- **Subsidies:** Available if income is $14,580 — $58,320/year (2024 for single person)
- **Cost with subsidies:** Often $0 — $150/month for a Silver plan
- **Apply:** healthcare.gov or your state's marketplace

**Key point:** If music income is low and you don't have employer coverage, you likely qualify for significant subsidies. Many artists pay $0-50/month.

### Option 3: Freelancers Union

- **Freelancers Union:** freelancersunion.org — not insurance itself, but connects freelancers (including musicians) to group plans and resources
- **National Association for the Self-Employed (NASE):** nase.org — group health benefits

### Option 4: Part-Time Job with Benefits

No shame in this. Many successful artists work part-time specifically for health benefits:
- Starbucks (20 hours/week = full benefits)
- UPS (part-time = health insurance)
- Costco (part-time benefits)
- Teaching music lessons at a school/studio (often includes benefits)

### Dental & Vision

Often overlooked but important:
- **Dental:** Standalone dental plans on healthcare.gov ($15-40/month)
- **Vision:** VSP or EyeMed individual plans ($10-20/month)
- **Dental schools:** Heavily discounted cleanings and procedures

### Burnout Signs to Watch For

Flag these if the artist exhibits them:

- Dreading making music (when they used to love it)
- Comparing themselves constantly to other artists
- Can't finish projects / starting but never completing
- Social media anxiety (obsessing over numbers)
- Physical symptoms: insomnia, appetite changes, fatigue
- Isolation from friends, family, scene
- Substance use increasing
- Cynicism about the industry ("it's all rigged")

### What to Say (and Not Say)

**When they're burned out:**
```
NOT: "Just keep grinding! Hustle culture!"
SAY: "It sounds like you need a week off. The music will still be there.
     The best creative work comes from a rested mind, not a depleted one.
     What would recharge you right now?"
```

**When they're comparing themselves:**
```
NOT: "Don't compare yourself to others!"
SAY: "I get it. But look at this — [specific thing they've accomplished].
     A year ago, that wasn't true. You're on a trajectory. Their timeline
     isn't yours, and you don't know what they sacrificed to get there."
```

**When they want to quit:**
```
NOT: "You can't quit! You're so talented!"
SAY: "What specifically is making you feel this way? Let's separate the
     frustration from the reality. Because there's a difference between
     'I hate the business' and 'I hate making music.' One is fixable."
```

**When they're spiraling on social media:**
```
SAY: "Delete Instagram from your phone for 48 hours. Not your account —
     just the app. Make music without looking at numbers. See how that feels.
     I'll handle anything that needs posting."
```

## Financial Safety Net

### The Artist Emergency Fund

Every artist should have:

1. **1 month of expenses saved** before spending on music (survival first)
2. **A separate music business account** (don't mix personal and music money)
3. **Track every expense** (the music-accounting skill handles this)
4. **Know your break-even** — how much do you need to earn monthly to sustain?

### Income Diversification

Don't rely on one stream:

```
INCOME SAFETY NET

Primary:     Streaming royalties ($___/mo)
Secondary:   Live shows ($___/mo average)
Tertiary:    Teaching/lessons ($___/mo)
Emergency:   Part-time work (if needed)
Growth:      Sync licensing (unpredictable but high-value)
Passive:     Beat sales / sample packs
```

**Goal:** No single income stream represents more than 50% of total. If streaming dried up tomorrow, could you survive on the rest?

## Life Infrastructure

### The Basics Checklist

Things that seem obvious but many independent artists don't have:

- [ ] Health insurance (even the cheapest ACA plan)
- [ ] Dental checkup in the last year
- [ ] Separate business bank account
- [ ] Estimated quarterly taxes being paid (or at least saved)
- [ ] Renter's insurance (covers equipment at home — often $15/month)
- [ ] Equipment insurance (if gear value exceeds $5K)
- [ ] Emergency fund: 1 month of expenses
- [ ] A will or basic estate plan (who gets your masters if something happens?)
- [ ] Up-to-date ID/passport (needed for international shows)
- [ ] Reliable transportation
- [ ] A workspace that isn't your bed

### Hearing Protection

Seriously. This is career-critical:

- **Custom molded earplugs:** $150-300 from an audiologist (worth every penny)
- **Budget option:** Etymotic ER20XS ($20) — flat frequency response, music sounds good
- **Rule:** Wear them at every show, every rehearsal, every loud session
- **Get a baseline hearing test:** Know where you start so you can track changes
- **Tinnitus is permanent.** Prevention is the only treatment.

## The Pep Talk Framework

When the artist needs perspective, draw from:

1. **Where they were vs where they are.** Always anchor to progress.
2. **Someone they admire who struggled.** Every career has a "before" that looked hopeless.
3. **What they can control.** Redirect from things they can't (algorithm, industry politics) to things they can (next song, next show, next connection).
4. **The math.** "If you release 12 songs this year and each grows your audience by 5%, you'll have 80% more listeners by December. That's just showing up."
5. **Permission to rest.** Rest is not quitting. Rest is maintenance on the machine.
